How the drawing routines take a different perspective of the landscape view

If you want to understand how the landscape is drawn in the Sentinel, then you can make it a lot easier to follow if you just think about the lanscape like this:

This is the landscape preview, and it's nice and easy to talk about. There are tile rows from left to right and tile columns from front to back, and tile coordinates follow along in a sensible manner: the x-coordinates go from 0 to 31 from left to right, while the z-coordinates go from 0 to 31 from front to back. Meanwhile, the y-coordinates determine altitude, with higher altitudes being higher up the screen. It's all very logical, and you can find out more in the deep dive on Cartesian coordinates.

This model feeds nicely into the drawing routines, which draw the landscape from back to front, so that near parts of the landscape can obscure further parts of the landscape (this is the so-called "painter's algorithm", because that's the order in which you would apply paint to a canvas to get a 3D effect). If we look at how the landscape preview is drawn, then we can see this in action:

You can see that in this case the landscape is drawn from the back row to the front, as that enables landscape in the front to appear in front of the landscape at the back.

But what happens if the camera is looking in a different direction? During gameplay the player can rotate left and right through 360 degrees, so what does this mean for concepts such as the "back row" and "moving forwards"?

The Sentinel addresses these concerns by defining quadrants and applying those to the algorithm in a number of places. To keep the deep dive on drawing the landscape view mamnageable, I've tried to explain the drawing process for the easy-to-grasp layout of the landscape preview, and I've extracted all the complicated quadrant logic into this article.

Let's start by talking about quadrants, and then we can look at how they affect the drawing algorithm.

Calculating the quadrant
------------------------

The first step when drawing the landscape is to work out which direction the player is looking at, so we can use that to determine how we draw the player's view. Specifically, we want to draw the landscape from the back, starting from the furthest row of tiles and working towards the player, and to do that we need to work out which tiles in the landscape are the furthest from the player in the direction in which they are looking.

More specifically, we need to work out which quadrant the player is looking in. To explain quadrants, let's consider the Sentinel perched on its tower above the landscape, like this:

What would its view look like, and how should we draw it? Well, if it's turned away from us and looking towards the back of the landscape in the above screenshot, then the correct drawing order would be the same as in the video - we should draw the rear row as the furthest row of tiles, and then work forwards, towards the Sentinel.

But if the Sentinel turns towards the right, then from its perspective, the furthest row of tiles is actually the rightmost column in the screenshot above, and to draw the landscape correctly we should start with that column of tiles, and work our way to the left until we reached the Sentinel. The same applies if the Sentinel is looking forwards (in which case we would start drawing from the front row of tiles) or looking to the left (in which case we would start drawing from the leftmost column).

The quadrant therefore defines which row or column of tiles we should be considering as the furthest line of tiles that need to appear at the back of the view we are going to draw. There are four choices, which in terms of the preview screen are the rear row, right column, front row or left column of tiles.

We calculate the quadrant from the player's yaw angle, which determines the direction in which they are looking. If we consider the layout of the landscape preview, then the player's yaw angle determines the direction of gaze, like this:

          0
    -32   |   +32         Overhead view of landscape
       \  |  /
        \ | /             0 = looking towards back row
         \|/              +64 = looking sharp right
 -64 -----+----- +64      -64 = looking sharp left
         /|\
        / | \             ^           x-axis left -->
       /  |  \            |             to right
    -96   |   +96         z-axis
         128              into screen

So if the player has a yaw angle of 0, then they are looking directly towards the back row of the landscape in the preview, while a yaw angle of 32 means they are looking at the far-right corner of the preview; see the deep dive on pitch and yaw angles for more details.

As well as yaw angle, we can think of the viewing direction in terms of a clock or a compass, but I prefer the clock terminology here as it's easier to adapt to the player's perspective (we can talk about something being at 3 o'clock or 9 o'clock from the perspective of the player, but this doesn't work quite so well if we're talking about east and west).

So to make this easier to describe, let's label the same diagram like a clock:

          12
  10.30   |    1.30
       \  |  /
        \ | /
         \|/
   9 -----+----- 3
         /|\
        / | \
       /  |  \
   7.30   |   4.30
          6

So if the player is looking at 12 o'clock, then they are looking towards the back row in the preview, while the far-right corner is at 1.30.

In terms of quadrants, then, the clock looks like this:

  10.30        1.30
       \     /
        \   /
         \ /
          +
         / \
        /   \
       /     \
   7.30       4.30

And to be more explicit, these are the four quadrants, in a clockwise order:

  • Quadrant arc: 10.30 to 1.30
    Centre of arc: 12 o'clock
  • Quadrant arc: 1.30 to 4.30
    Centre of arc: 3 o'clock
  • Quadrant arc: 4.30 to 7.30
    Centre of arc: 6 o'clock
  • Quadrant arc: 7.30 to 10.30
    Centre of arc: 9 o'clock

To convert the player's yaw angle into a quadrant, we take the player's yaw angle and add 45 degrees to it, storing the result in the viewingArcRightYaw variable. This rotates the quadrant diagram above into the more familiar clock quarters, like this:

          12
          |
          |
          |
          |
   9 -----+----- 3
          |
          |
          |
          |
          6

So we can work out which quadrant contains the player's gaze by working out which quarter contains the yaw angle in viewingArcRightYaw. And we can do this by simply looking at bits 6 and 7 of our new angle, because that's how the angles work:

                  0
  -64 to -1       |     0 to 63
  =  %11000000    |     =  %00000000
  to %11111111    |     to %00111111
                  |
         -64 -----+----- +64
                  |
  -128 to -65     |     64 to 127
  =  %10000000    |     =  %01000000
  to %10111111    |     to %01111111
                -128

We then rotate anticlockwise by 45 degrees to convert this back into the relevant quadrant, so we get the following:

  • viewingArcRightYaw has bit 6 = 0, bit 7 = 0, quadrant is 10.30 to 1.30
  • viewingArcRightYaw has bit 6 = 1, bit 7 = 0, quadrant is 1.30 to 4.30
  • viewingArcRightYaw has bit 6 = 0, bit 7 = 1, quadrant is 4.30 to 7.30
  • viewingArcRightYaw has bit 6 = 1, bit 7 = 1, quadrant is 7.30 to 10.30

If you're having trouble visualising this, then imagine a dog on the landscape. And now imagine that this dog has been to the vert after a tumble, and is having to wear one of those Elizabethan collars to stop her licking her wounds while they heal (see this entry on Wikipedia if you don't know what I mean). Finally, let's say that the the viewing arc for our poor injured pup is exactly 90 degrees from the left to the right side of her view.

If we take the angle in which the dog is looking - the dog's yaw angle - and add 45 degrees, then we get the yaw angle along the right edge of the dog's cone. This angle is what we stored in the viewingArcRightYaw variable above (hence the variable name).

Now consider our clock again:

          12
  10.30   |    1.30
       \  |  /
        \ | /
         \|/
   9 -----+----- 3
         /|\
        / | \
       /  |  \
   7.30   |   4.30
          6

Let's imagine our puppy scanning the landscape for her owner. First of all, she scans the top quadrant, looking between 10.30 and 1.30. When she's looking at 10.30, the right edge of the cone is pointing at 12 o'clock, and when she's looking at 1.30, the right edge is at 3 o'clock. So when viewingArcRightYaw is between 12 o'clock and 3 o'clock (i.e. when bits 6 and 7 are clear), the dog is looking in the top quadrant, between 10.30 and 1.30.

Similarly, when the dog is scanning between 1.30 and 4.30, the right edge points to anywhere between 3 o'clock and 6 o'clock, which is when bit 7 is clear and bit 6 is set. And if we repeat the process for all four quadrants, we get the result we are after:

  • viewingArcRightYaw has bit 6 = 0, bit 7 = 0, quadrant is 10.30 to 1.30
  • viewingArcRightYaw has bit 6 = 1, bit 7 = 0, quadrant is 1.30 to 4.30
  • viewingArcRightYaw has bit 6 = 0, bit 7 = 1, quadrant is 4.30 to 7.30
  • viewingArcRightYaw has bit 6 = 1, bit 7 = 1, quadrant is 7.30 to 10.30

This gives us the quadrant in which the player is looking, so let's see what we can do with this information.

Using the quadrant
------------------

Part 1 of DrawLandscapeView sets up a collection of variables that record the quadrant in a variety of ways, and these variables influence the way in which the landscape is drawn. It also calculates the tile coordinate of the viewer, but with the axes rotated to match the orientation of the viewer, so the x- and z-coordinate axes are from the perspective of the viewer rather than of the 3D world.

The quadrant variables are as follows:

  • viewingArcRightYaw is the variable we described above, which contains the player's yaw angle plus 45 degrees to give the right edge of the 90-degree viewing arc with the player's gaze at the centre.
  • quadrantOffset is set to the offset that we need to add to a point index within the drawing tables if we want to move by one corner in the direction of the quadrant's gaze.
  • viewingQuadrantx4 is set to the quadrant number but multiplied by 4, for use when calculating the number of faces in a tile in DrawSlopingTile.
  • viewingQuadrantOpp is set to the opposite quadrant number to that containing the player's gaze.
  • screenLeftYawHi is set to the yaw angle of the gaze in the centre of the viewing arc, less 14.0625 degrees (i.e. 360 * 10 / 256). The screen is 20 yaw angle units across, so this sets screenLeftYawHi to the high byte of the yaw angle of the left edge of the screen, reduced into the range of a single 90-degree quadrant (so it's relative to the 90-degree viewing arc).

Don't worry if these don't make sense - the main takeway is that there's a bunch of variables that capture the direction of the player's gaze. And the clever bit is this: these variables effectively rotate the landscape through the correct multiple of 90 degrees such that we always draw the landscape from the back row to the front row.

To see what I mean, take the landscape preview and put it on one of those rotating cake stands:

If the player is looking at the 10.30 to 1.30 quadrant, then we draw the landscape from the back row to the front row, just like in the clip above.

If the player is looking at the 1.30 to 4.30 quadrant, i.e. to the right in the above screenshot, then we can rotate the preview anticlockwise by 90 degrees so that the rightmost tile column moves around to become the row of tiles at the back. And then we can draw the landscape from the back row to the front row, which will actually draw the landscape from the rightmost column to the leftmost column.

If the player is looking at the 4.30 to 7.30 quadrant, i.e. staight into the camera in the above screenshot, then we can rotate the preview by 180 degrees so that the front row moves to the back. And then we can draw the landscape from the back row to the front row, which from the perspective of the player will from the furthest row to the nearest.

And finally, if the player is looking at the 7.30 to 10.30 quadrant, i.e. to the left in the above screenshot, then we can rotate the preview clockwise by 90 degrees so that the leftmost tile column moves to the back, so when we can draw the landscape from the back row to the front row, which will actually draw the landscape from the left column to the right.

There is another thing we need to do before we can move on to the drawing routines, and that's to work out the tile coordinates of the player, but within the rotated landscape that we're going to be working with. If we don't need to rotate the landscape via our quadrant variables (when the player is looking between 10.30 and 1.30), then the player's tile coordinate is fine as it is. But if we are going to be drawing the landscape in a different direction, we need to convert the player's tile coordinates as follows:

  • If the viewer has not turned: (x, z) maps to (x, z)
  • If the viewer has turned right: (x, z) maps to (30 - z, x)
  • If the viewer has turned around: (x, z) maps to (30 - x, 30 - z)
  • If the viewer has turned left: (x, z) maps to (z, 30 - x)

Let's take the second example, where the viewer is looking to the right, into the quadrant from 1.30 to 4.30. If you imagine the standard 3D world, with the z-axis going into the screen and the x-axis going from left to right, then turning right means that from our new perspective:

  • The axis we now see running from left to right is the 3D world z-axis, but in the opposite direction.
  • The axis we now see going away from us is the 3D world x-axis.

Therefore, from the perspective of the viewer:

  • The x-axis is the 3D world z-axis in the opposite direction, which is 30 - z.
  • The z-axis is the 3D world x-axis, which is x.

So that's what we calculate for the player's tile coordinate relative to the orientation, putting the resulting coordinate into the varables xTileViewer and zTileViewer, and we use a similar calculation for the other two quadrants.

Throughout the drawing routines, the assumption is that we have set up all these variables so that the landscape is effectively around to the correct orientation to be drawing the row at the back and moving forwards, even if, in the original landscape, that row is a column. Sometimes this can give us a challenge, as in part 2 of GetTileViewAngles, where we have a viewer-relative tile coordinate for which we need to fetch the tile data. The tileData table stores tiles in the same way as the landscape preview, so before we can fetch the data we need to convert the viewer-relative tile coordinate into a coordinate that is suitable for looking up tile data. But for most of the drawing, we can imagine that we are drawing distant rows first and moving forwards, even if that isn't strictly true.

That's quite enough on quadrants and orientation, so let's move on to the drawing process itself.
