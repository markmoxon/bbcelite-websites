Representing energy levels with robot, tree and boulder icons

The top row of the game screen contains two important status indicators: on the left is the player's energy level, and on the right is the scanner. We'll take a quick look at the energy level indicator here, while its companion is covered in its own deep dive on the scanner.

The energy icon and scanner row can be seen along the top of the screen, just above the player's scrolling landscape view:

Energy levels are indicated by a sequence of icons that together add up to the player's energy level. Each icon represents the amount of energy captured in each type of object (i.e. the amount of energy required to create that object, or the amount of energy you get if you absorb that object). The in-game energy levels are defined in the objectTypeEnergy table, but only three types of object appear in the energy row, along with another non-game version of the robot.

Here are the objects that can appear in the energy row (using the palette for landscape 0000):

So in the above screenshot, the player's total energy level is two robots and a boulder, which comes to 3 + 3 + 2 = 8 energy points. The player always starts with ten energy points (three robots and a tree), and the maximum amount of energy in a landscape is 49 energy points, so in theory the maximum level we will ever have to display is 59 points (that's three high-energy robots, one normal robot and a tree). Incidentally, you will never see all the icons on the screen at any one time, as you never see a tree and a boulder together (because tree + boulder = robot).

The energy level can be updated by poking the correct sequence of sprites into the dedicated screen buffer. This icon screen buffer is described in the deep dive on screen buffers, but essentially we can call the ShowIconBuffer routine to display the contents of the icon screen buffer by copying it into screen memory.

We have to update the top row via a screen buffer because the screen uses hardware scrolling to scroll the landscape view, and this works by changing the address of the start of screen memory. This means that the scrolling process will also scroll the top row of the screen, so the address of the energy icons and scanner row is not fixed and changes with the different scroll positions. The GetIconRowAddress routine can be used to calculate the address of the energy icon character row in screen memory - it just takes the address of the landscape view from viewScreenAddr(1 0) and subtracts 320 to move up one row (as there are 320 bytes in each character row). But it's a lot easier just to update the buffer and call ShowIconBuffer.

The top bar can be cleared by calling the ClearIconsScanner routine, which simply draws a sequence of blank icons into the buffer. The energy icons are updated by the UpdateIconsScanner routine, which clears the entire row before drawing the required number of energy icons (and it also draws an empty scanner box on the right).

UpdateIconsScanner calculates the required icons by taking the energy level and working through the icons from high energy robot down to tree, checking at each stage to see whether the energy level is bigger than the current icon. If it is, then we draw an icon of that type and subtract the energy level from the total, and then we repeat the process; if the energy level is lower than the icon being considered, we drop down to the next icon down in the energy list and try that one, until we have processed the tree at the bottom of the pile.

Each of these icons can be drawn by calling the DrawIcon routine, passing the required icon type in A. Each individual icon is one character block in size, so that's eight pixels high and four pixels wide; this means that all the energy icons apart from the robot require two or more parts - the tree icon is five pixels wide, for example, so it is made up of two smaller icons. The UpdateIconsScanner routine calls DrawIcons twice for each energy icon, but for the robots it draws a blank icon in the second position. This ensures that the icons are evenly spaced.

The data for each icon is stored in the iconData table, where the pixels in each icon are stored as standard mode 5 pixel bytes (see the deep dive on the custom screen mode for details).

Each energy icon are always drawn as two icons, but the scanner is a little more complicated; it consists of ten character blocks in total, with one block at each end for the caps, and eight character blocks in the middle, which is where the statis is shown when the player is spotted by an enemy.
